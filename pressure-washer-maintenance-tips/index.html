The most common killer: pump neglect

The pump is the heart of any pressure washer. Most axial cam pumps on consumer machines last about 100–200 hours before seals start leaking if oil is never changed. A new pump costs $80–250, but a full rebuild kit is often under $30. Replacing worn seals every 100 hours is cheaper than buying a whole new unit. Check the pump oil level every 10 hours of use; change it every 50 hours or once per season. Use non-detergent 30-weight pump oil or the manufacturer’s recommended fluid. A small puddle under the unit means the seals are failing — catch it early and replace them before water gets into the crankcase and destroys the bearings.

How to change pump oil

Turn the machine off, disconnect spark plug (if gas), and tip the unit so the oil drain plug is at the lowest point. Remove the plug and let old oil drain into a container. Reinstall plug, fill through the dipstick hole until oil reaches the “full” line. Wipe any spills. Do this with the pump warm for best flow. Never overfill — excess pressure can blow seals out.

Engine and motor maintenance

Gas engines (Honda, Briggs & Stratton, Predator) are reliable if you treat them right. Dirty air filters reduce power and let grit into the cylinder. Clean the foam pre-filter after 25 hours; replace paper filter every 100 hours or yearly. Change engine oil every 50 hours or once a season — use SAE 10W-30 for most small engines. Stale fuel is the #1 reason carburetors fail; use ethanol-free gas and add a fuel stabilizer for any machine that sits more than 30 days. For electric motors, blow out dust from vents with compressed air every 3 months. Check the power cord for nicks — a damaged cord can short the motor.

Spark plug replacement schedule

Inspect the spark plug annually. If the electrode is rounded or the porcelain is cracked, replace it (about $5–8). Gap to 0.030 inches for most engines. A weak spark makes starting hard and wastes fuel.

Water system care: hoses, nozzles, and filters

Garden hoses should be 3/4-inch minimum diameter to supply enough water flow; a 50-foot 5/8-inch hose robs 20–30% of the machine’s potential. Use a quick-connect filter at the pump inlet to catch sediment — clean the screen after each use. Pressure hoses are rated for thousands of psi, but kinking or sharp bends weaken the inner tube. Store the hose loosely coiled, never hung over a nail which creates a pinch point. Nozzles wear out faster than you think: a 0-degree nozzle can erode to a 30-degree pattern after 20 hours of use on abrasive surfaces. Replace worn nozzles because they reduce cleaning power and force the pump to work harder. Flush the chemical injector after every use with clean water — leftover soap dries and clogs the siphon tube.

Winterization and storage

Freezing water is the fastest way to crack a pump or burst a hose. Before any freeze, run the machine with RV antifreeze (non-toxic) through the system for 30 seconds. Or, drain all water: disconnect hoses, pull the recoil starter slowly to expel water from the pump, tip the unit to drain the pump housing. Store the machine in a dry area above 32°F. Remove the spark plug and squirt a teaspoon of engine oil into the cylinder, then pull the starter a few times to coat the cylinder walls. This prevents rust during months of inactivity. For long-term storage (over 3 months), drain the fuel tank or run it dry, then add fresh fuel next season.

A simple maintenance schedule to follow

Interval Task
After each use Flush soap system, rinse nozzles, wipe down machine, inspect hoses
Every 10 hours Check pump oil level, clean inlet filter screen
Every 25 hours Clean engine air filter foam pre-filter
Every 50 hours Change engine oil, change pump oil, inspect spark plug
Annually (or end of season) Replace fuel filter, change pump seals if leaking, winterize
Every 2 years Replace high-pressure hose, replace spark plug, inspect pump bearings

Stick to this schedule and your pressure washer will still be running strong when cheaper units have long since hit the landfill. The $50–100 you spend per year on oil, filters, and seals is a fraction of a new machine’s cost. More importantly, your machine will start on the first pull and deliver full pressure every time you need it.

Pro tip: keep a log

Write down the hours you use the machine and note when you perform each task. An index card taped to the frame works. This makes it easy to track and proves you’ve done the work if you ever need warranty service. A little record keeping is the difference between a machine that dies at 5 years and one that passes the 10-year mark.
