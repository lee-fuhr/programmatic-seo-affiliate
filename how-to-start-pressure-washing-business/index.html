The honest startup guide to a pressure washing business

If you’re looking to start a pressure washing business, you’ve probably seen the flashy ads: “Make $200 an hour with a $300 machine!” It sounds easy, but the reality is different. I’ve been running my own pressure washing company for five years, and I want to give you the straight talk — no hype, no undersell. This guide covers the minimum gear, realistic startup costs, how to price your work legally and honestly, and a step-by-step plan for landing your first ten customers.

Equipment to start your business: Commercial pressure washer → Surface cleaner → 50-ft hose →

What you actually need (minimum equipment list with approximate costs)

You don’t need a $5,000 setup to start. But you also don’t want to buy a toy that breaks after three jobs. Here’s the honest minimum:

  • Gas-powered pressure washer (residential grade). Look for a unit with at least 2.5 gallons per minute (GPM) and around 3,000–3,500 PSI. A model like the Simpson Cleaning MSH3125 MegaShot or a comparable unit from Craftsman or DeWalt will run you about $350–$500. Avoid electric units — they’re too slow for anything but a single car.
  • Surface cleaner (12–16 inch). A surface cleaner attaches to your wand and lets you clean flat concrete in half the time. A good entry-level model costs $120–$180. Brands like Whisper Wash or Suttner are reliable.
  • Extension wand (18–24 inches). For high work or tight spaces. Expect $30–$50.
  • Three quick-connect spray tips (0°, 25°, 40°). Usually included with the washer, but buy a spare set for $15.
  • Heavy-duty hose (50 feet, 3/8-inch diameter). The hose that comes with most cheaper washers is too short and kinks easily. A good rubber hose is $60–$100.
  • Soap nozzle and downstream injector. Most gas washers have a detergent tank built in, but some need a separate injector. Budget $20–$40.
  • Business-grade cleaning chemicals. You need a quality degreaser and a soft-wash solution for low-pressure cleaning. A gallon of concentrated degreaser (e.g., Zep Heavy Duty) runs $20–$30. You’ll dilute it, so it lasts a while.
  • Personal protective equipment (PPE). Safety glasses ($10–$15), heavy rubber gloves ($10), and high-ankle waterproof boots ($50–$80).
  • Transport and storage. A 5-gallon bucket for soap ($5), a pressure washer cart or a simple dolly ($50–$80) if you don’t have a truck. Or just strap it in your trunk — it’s not ideal, but it works.

Total minimum cost: roughly $700 to $1,100. This gets you a functional rig that won’t embarrass you on a job. You will also need a business license and insurance, which I’ll discuss below.

Pricing strategy: how to not rent yourself out for peanuts

There’s no one-size-fits-all price. I can’t tell you “charge $200 per driveway” because markets vary wildly. But I can give you a framework that’s honest. Your price should cover four things: your hourly operating cost, your desired income, overhead, and profit margin.

First, calculate your minimum hourly cost. That includes fuel, chemicals, equipment wear, and insurance. For a small rig, figure $10–$15 per hour of operation. Then decide what you want to take home. A reasonable goal for a solo startup is $30–$50 per hour after expenses. That sounds low compared to social media claims, but it’s real. Your first year, you’ll be slow, inefficient, and doing a lot of free quotes. Aim for $60–$80 per hour billed as a baseline. For a typical driveway (1–2 hours), that means $120–$160. A house washing (1–3 hours) should be $200–$400. A deck or fence (2–4 hours) $200–$450.

Don’t charge by the hour for customers — quote by the job. But use your hourly target to set your quotes. Be transparent. Tell customers: “Most two-car driveways in this area run $125–$175. I can give you a firm price after I see the job.” Honesty builds trust.

And here’s the truth: you will not be making six figures your first year. You might gross $15,000–$30,000 if you work part-time, $40,000–$60,000 full-time with solid hustle. Costs (gas, insurance, maintenance, marketing) will eat 20–30% of that. Keep your overhead low.

Getting your first 10 customers (the honest way)

No influencer strategy. No “hack.” Here is exactly what worked for me and many others in my local network.

  1. Do free or deeply discounted work for high-visibility homes. Find a friend with a really dirty driveway on a busy street. Offer to clean it for $40. Take before-and-after photos. Put a sign in the yard: “Pressure washing by [Your Business Name] — call for a free quote.” That first job costs you time, but it becomes your best ad. Aim for three such jobs on different streets.
  2. Knock on doors (or use the “flyer on the mailbox” method). Create a simple flyer with one photo of a clean driveway and your phone number. Walk a neighborhood that looks like it hasn’t been cleaned in years. Hang a flyer on each door (or put in mailbox if legal in your area). Say: “I’m working on your street today. If you want a $50 discount on a full driveway wash, call by 5pm.” The discount is real. You lose $50 but gain a customer who will leave a review.
  3. Use Nextdoor and local Facebook groups. Post a genuine offer: “I’m a new pressure washing business on the north side. I’m offering the first 10 customers a 20% discount in exchange for an honest Google review. Message me for a free quote.” Do not spam. And follow up fast.
  4. Partner with a real estate agent. Agents often need driveways and sidewalks cleaned before listing a home. Offer a flat $20 referral fee or do one job for them free. Introduce yourself to three agents at open houses. Most will say no. One might say yes.
  5. Leave business cards at local hardware stores, car washes, and laundromats. Don’t pay for advertising. Just cards in a holder. Someone buying a new pressure washer at Home Depot might need you to clean their rental property.

After ten jobs, you’ll have at least six or seven Google reviews and a few photos. That’s your foundation. Then you can consider a simple website or a Google Business Profile (which is free and mandatory).

Basic business setup (the boring but essential stuff)

Don’t skip this. If you get sued or fined, your startup dies. Here’s what you need before you take a paid job:

  • Business license (city or county). Usually $50–$200 per year. Just call your local city hall or check their website. Don’t guess. You need a license to operate legally.
  • General liability insurance. This is non-negotiable. A small policy ( $1 million per occurrence ) costs $400–$800 per year for a pressure washing business. I recommend Next Insurance or Hiscox — you can get a policy online in ten minutes. Do not start without it. One cracked window or water intrusion could cost you thousands.
  • Simple contract/waiver. You don’t need a lawyer yet. Download a free template online for “pressure washing service agreement.” Modify it to say: “Customer agrees to repair any pre-existing damage, and I am not responsible for damage due to weak mortar or loose paint.” Have the customer sign before you start. Yes, it’s awkward. Do it anyway.
  • Business bank account. Free at most local banks. Keep your money separate from personal funds. It makes tax time much easier.
