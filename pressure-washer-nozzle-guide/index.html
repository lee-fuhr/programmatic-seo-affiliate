How Pressure Washer Nozzle Tips Work

A pressure washer nozzle does two things: meters the water flow (via a precisely sized orifice) and shapes the stream into a fan pattern. The angle of that fan is what gives each tip its color and its cleaning personality. Wider angles spread the force over a larger area, which reduces the chance of etching or gouging but also lowers the effective pressure at the surface. Narrower angles focus all that energy into a tight jet, stripping paint and blasting mud but also risking damage.

The standard color coding follows the international ANSI/AI standard, so you can trust that a red tip from any brand works the same way. Below is the conventional breakdown, but remember: your actual cleaning speed and safety depend on your machine’s flow (GPM) and pressure (PSI) as much as the nozzle angle. A 4000 PSI machine with a 40-degree tip still packs more punch than a 1500 PSI machine with a 0-degree tip.

The Five Standard Nozzle Colors and Their Angles

Color Spray Angle Primary Use
Red 0° Stubborn stain removal, paint stripping, tough grease
Yellow 15° Heavy-duty cleaning of concrete, masonry, brick, and metal
Green 25° General-purpose cleaning: siding, driveways, decks, fences
White 40° Light cleaning, rinsing, applying detergent, washing cars
Black 65° (or soap tip) Low-pressure detergent or chemical application

Note: Some manufacturers offer a brown or gray tip for 50° or 60° spray patterns, but red through white covers 90% of residential and light commercial work. The black tip is special: it has a much larger orifice to drop pressure and allow soap pickup (if your machine has a siphon tube).

Red Nozzle (0°): Chisel, Not a Spray

A 0-degree tip produces a solid straight jet with no fan. On a typical 3000 PSI machine, the striking force at 1 inch from the surface is roughly the same as the machine’s pump output—3000 PSI concentrated into a pinhead-sized area. Move that jet across concrete and you can cut through oil stains, but hold it still and you’ll etch a groove. Use this tip only on hard, unyielding surfaces like concrete, steel, or stone, and keep it moving. Never use a 0-degree tip on wood, car paint, or soft siding—it will gouge in milliseconds.

Yellow Nozzle (15°): The Stripper

The 15-degree tip spreads the stream into a narrow fan about as wide as your finger at close range. It’s the go-to for removing old paint, flaking coatings, and heavy mildew from concrete or brick. Because the fan is still tight, you can tackle gummed-up brick pavers or weathered fence boards without leaving visible lines—provided you hold the nozzle at least six inches away and move in consistent passes. If your pressure washer runs at 3.5 GPM, the yellow tip will clean about 50% faster than the green tip on the same surface, but it also has a much lower margin for error. Never use it on asphalt shingles, softwood (cedar, redwood), or painted metal.

Green Nozzle (25°): The Workhorse

This is the tip most homeowners should keep on their gun by default. At 25 degrees, the fan is wide enough to cover a decent strip of siding or driveway (about 6–8 inches at 12 inches of standoff) while still delivering enough force to blast away dirt and loose paint. On a 2.5 GPM / 3200 PSI machine, the green tip applies roughly 1,000–1,300 PSI to the surface—strong enough to clean concrete and wood deck boards (with caution) but not so aggressive that it will tear up good paint or dent aluminum siding. Use the green nozzle for routine house washing, driveway cleaning, and outdoor furniture.

White Nozzle (40°): The Rinser and Car Washer

The 40-degree tip produces a wide, gentle fan that drops the effective pressure to about 400–600 PSI (on a 3200 PSI machine). This is perfect for rinsing detergent off cars, washing glass and windows, cleaning outdoor curtains, or giving a final pass on painted wood to avoid streaks. The white tip also works well for applying cleaning solutions that don’t need a high-pressure blast to activate. Because the spray is so soft, you can move the wand slowly without worrying about damage. On a low-flow electric pressure washer (1.2 GPM), the white tip may feel weak—hold the nozzle closer (6–8 inches) to get the cleaning power you need.

Black Soap Nozzle (65°): Detergent Duty

This tip is not meant for pressure washing at all. Its wide, low-pressure spray is strictly for applying soap or chemical cleaners. The orifice is large (often 0.060″ or bigger) which drops the system pressure to roughly 200–400 PSI—too low to remove dirt, but enough to wet a surface evenly. Use it with your pressure washer’s onboard detergent injector or with a separate foam cannon connector. Some machines include a dedicated soap nozzle
